# Evidence Rubric

## Evidence ladder

Use this ladder for every non-trivial claim.

### Level A. direct evidence
The claim is directly supported by the source.
Examples:
- the paper states the dataset size,
- the lab website lists the student as a PhD student,
- the official proceedings confirm the venue.

### Level B. strong indirect evidence
The claim is not stated once, but repeated signals strongly support it.
Examples:
- the same person appears as first author across several years and is listed on the lab site,
- the same external collaborator appears across a long research line.

### Level C. reasonable inference
The claim is plausible but not fully confirmed.
Examples:
- likely compute intensity inferred from model scale and experiment scope,
- likely advising pattern inferred from repeated student authorship growth.

### Level D. speculation
The claim is weakly supported and should be labeled as speculative.
Examples:
- lab culture inferred from a single conference burst,
- personality traits inferred from writing style alone.

## Confidence scale

### High
Use only when the judgment relies mostly on Level A or repeated Level B evidence.

### Medium
Use when the judgment combines some direct evidence with reasonable inferences, or when the evidence is consistent but incomplete.

### Low
Use when the judgment depends on sparse, noisy, or conflicting evidence.

## Counter-evidence protocol

For any major judgment, provide five items:

- **claim**: the judgment being made.
- **supporting evidence**: cite specific papers, sources, or patterns.
- **alternative explanations** (minimum 2-3): present at least two plausible alternative interpretations for ambiguous signals. For example:
  - High publication rate could mean: (1) productive and well-resourced lab, (2) paper-slicing culture, (3) large group with many students contributing independently.
  - Few student first-author papers could mean: (1) professor dominates authorship, (2) students are early-stage and not yet ready, (3) field convention differs.
  - Frequent collaboration with industry could mean: (1) strong applied impact, (2) funding dependency, (3) students get real-world exposure.
- **weighted judgment**: after presenting alternatives, state which explanation you consider most likely and why, with a rough confidence weight.
- **what would reduce uncertainty**: specific actions or information that could distinguish between the alternatives.

### Ambiguity threshold rule
If supporting evidence and counter-evidence are roughly equally strong, do NOT default to the positive interpretation. State the ambiguity explicitly and recommend the user investigate further (e.g., talk to current students, read lab news, check Glassdoor-like sources).

## High-risk inference areas

Apply extra caution to these topics:
- mentoring quality,
- workload or overwork,
- student contribution share,
- lab funding level,
- ethics or scientific integrity,
- interpersonal style.

## Mandatory language cues

Use phrases like:
- “directly supported by”
- “suggested by repeated evidence”
- “reasonable inference”
- “cannot be concluded from public evidence alone”
